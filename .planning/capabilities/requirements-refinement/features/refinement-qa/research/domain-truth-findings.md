## Domain Truth Findings

### First Principles

- **Sequential decision-making exhibits anchoring bias: earlier resolutions influence later ones.** In sequential review tasks, human decision-makers are measurably influenced by their previous decisions. After accepting 3 items in a row, they become more likely to reject the next; after rejecting 3, more likely to accept. Research shows AI-determined ordering reduces sequential dependency by 0.07 (Pearson r) and increases agreement with ground truth by 7%. For refinement-qa, this means the priority ordering from RECOMMENDATIONS.md will shape user decisions beyond the item content itself. High-severity items presented first will anchor the user's accept/reject threshold for everything that follows. This is unavoidable in any sequential walkthrough -- the mitigation is awareness, not prevention. — [https://dl.acm.org/doi/fullHtml/10.1145/3491102.3517443]

- **Decision fatigue degrades judgment quality over the course of a review session, and the degradation is non-linear.** Research on judicial parole decisions, medical diagnosis, and exam performance consistently shows that decision quality drops with repeated decisions, with a sharp recovery after breaks. Judges granted parole at ~65% at session start, dropping to near 0% before a food break, then resetting. For refinement-qa, this means items at the end of a long agenda will receive less cognitive engagement regardless of their importance. The "all items discussed regardless of severity" decision (EU-01) is correct for thoroughness but creates a tension: thoroughness vs. decision quality. The longer the agenda, the worse the later decisions. — [https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6119549/]

- **Resolution ordering in dependent-decision hierarchies must respect causal structure, not just priority.** Research on decision prioritization in hierarchies shows humans exhibit a bias toward resolving uncertainty locally -- they make categorical decisions at each node before proceeding deeper, reflecting working memory constraints. Optimal strategies often require the opposite: resolving lower-level uncertainties first. For refinement-qa, this means the resolution sequence from RECOMMENDATIONS.md may need to present dependency-blocked items in causal order (resolve the dependency first, then the dependent), even when the dependent item has higher severity. Priority ordering that violates causal dependencies will produce decisions the user may need to revisit. — [First principles: this extends the topological ordering constraint identified in coherence-report domain research. The Q&A walkthrough inherits this constraint from the resolution sequence it consumes. If coherence-report orders by severity alone, refinement-qa will present items in a causally invalid order, forcing revisits in the open-ended phase.]

- **The semi-structured interview (structured phase followed by open-ended phase) is the validated hybrid for guided decision-making.** Research methodology literature consistently validates the structured-then-open pattern: structured questions establish a shared baseline of facts, then open-ended questions surface unanticipated concerns. The key finding is that the transition must be explicit -- participants need a clear signal that the interaction mode is changing. Without this signal, users either (a) attempt to answer open-ended prompts with the same constrained format, or (b) attempt to raise new topics during the structured phase, disrupting the agenda. The refinement-qa two-phase design (FN-02 structured, FN-03 open-ended) maps directly to this validated pattern. — [https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6910737/; https://dovetail.com/research/semi-structured-interview/]

### Universal Constraints

- **Three resolution options is near the cognitive optimum for per-item decisions under sequential load.** Cognitive load research shows that decision quality degrades with option count, particularly under sequential task conditions. Binary (accept/reject) loses information; 5+ options cause choice overload. The 3-option structure (accept / research-needed / reject-modify) maps to the minimum viable disposition set used across inspection methodologies: accept the finding as stated, defer for more information, or actively disagree. Fagan inspection uses a similar structure (rework, verify, accept) in its resolution phase. Adding more entry types (ASSUMPTION_OVERRIDE, USER_INITIATED) as output categories rather than per-item choices is the correct separation -- the user picks from 3, the system classifies the output into 6. — [First principles: option count for per-item decisions should be minimized for the decision-maker while output granularity should be maximized for the consumer. These are independent concerns. The user decides intent (3 options); the system maps intent to structured types (6 types). This is the standard pattern in triage systems -- medical triage uses 3-5 acuity levels for intake but maps to dozens of downstream disposition codes.]

- **Revisiting previous decisions is cognitively costly and users are reluctant to do it, even when they should.** Anchoring research shows respondents are reluctant to change their opinion once stated. This is compounded by commitment bias -- having publicly committed to a decision (even to an AI), humans resist revising it. For refinement-qa's open-ended phase (FN-03), the "revisit a structured decision" capability is necessary but will be underutilized. Users will exit Q&A with some suboptimal decisions intact simply because revisiting feels like admitting error. The system cannot force revisits, but it can lower the friction: presenting a summary of all decisions before exit gives users a low-cost opportunity to spot issues without explicitly "revisiting." — [https://dl.acm.org/doi/fullHtml/10.1145/3491102.3517443; First principles: commitment escalation is a well-documented cognitive bias. The mitigation is to provide a review checkpoint that frames revision as normal workflow rather than error correction.]

- **Machine-readable change artifacts require both structured fields and human-readable reasoning to serve dual consumers.** Change control literature consistently shows that purely machine-readable formats (JSON, strict schemas) lose the reasoning context needed for human review, while purely narrative formats resist automated processing. The hybrid approach -- markdown with structured fields (type, source, capabilities, action) plus free-text reasoning -- is the validated pattern for artifacts consumed by both humans (during review) and machines (during change-application). The CHANGESET.md format spec (TC-02) correctly adopts this hybrid. The critical invariant: reasoning fields must never be optional, even for ACCEPT entries. An accept without reasoning ("I agree because...") becomes opaque to future reviewers and to the refinement-artifact's delta tracking. — [https://www.deltaxignia.com/blog/document-management/the-finddecidetrace-framework-building-reliable-change-control-in-an-age-of-complexity-and-ai; https://keepachangelog.com/en/1.1.0/]

### Validated Assumptions

- **LLM-based conversational agents successfully administer structured questionnaires with high completion rates, but struggle with user-initiated deviations.** A 2025 study on LLM survey agents achieved 98% response extraction accuracy and 100% completion across 40 surveys, but participants noted the agent failed to intelligently skip questions based on prior context and struggled to accommodate answer revisions. This validates that the structured Q&A phase (FN-02) will work well for walking the agenda, but the transition to open-ended phase (FN-03) and the revisit capability are the hard parts. The agent prompt must explicitly handle mode-switching and revision requests. — [https://arxiv.org/html/2504.02891v1]

- **"Too many similar type questions lead to participant fatigue" -- item count matters for engagement quality.** Research on LLM-administered questionnaires found that repetitive question patterns cause disengagement, manifesting as shorter responses and lower-quality answers. For refinement-qa, if the coherence-report produces 20+ agenda items of similar severity and type, the user will disengage on later items. The mitigation is grouping: present related items together rather than interleaving unrelated items, so the user's mental context stays loaded. The coherence-report's root-cause clustering (grouping symptoms under shared causes) already serves this purpose if the Q&A agenda inherits the grouping. — [https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/3719160.3736606; https://arxiv.org/html/2504.02891v1]

- **The upstream coherence-report's low-recall contradiction detection means refinement-qa must compensate.** The coherence-report domain research established that LLMs are high-precision/low-recall on contradiction detection (~45% for conditional contradictions). This means the Q&A agenda will systematically under-represent contradictions. The open-ended phase (FN-03) is the natural compensation point -- the user's domain knowledge catches contradictions the LLM missed. But this only works if the open-ended phase explicitly invites contradiction surfacing ("Are there any findings you think conflict with each other?") rather than a generic "anything else?" prompt. — [coherence-report domain-truth-findings.md; https://arxiv.org/html/2504.00180v1]

### Domain Risks

- **Decision fatigue on long agendas will cause users to default-accept later items.** Research shows that under decision fatigue, people "prefer a passive role" and "demonstrate passive behavior during subsequent decision-making." In a 3-option system, the passive choice is ACCEPT (requires no reasoning input). For refinement-qa, this means a 15+ item agenda will produce a tail of unreflective acceptances. The risk is not that acceptances are wrong -- the recommendations may be good -- but that the user didn't actually deliberate on them. Downstream, change-application will execute changes the user rubber-stamped. Possible mitigations: batch items into groups of 5-7 with natural break points, or surface a "you've accepted the last N items -- still engaged?" checkpoint. — [https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6119549/; First principles: the accept option is the lowest-cognitive-cost choice, making it the default under fatigue. This is analogous to the "agree to all cookies" pattern.]

- **The "all items discussed" invariant creates a tension with session length that has no clean resolution.** The decision to discuss every item regardless of severity (EU-01) is correct for thoroughness but conflicts with the decision fatigue constraint. Skipping low-severity items risks missing user-relevant issues; discussing them all risks fatiguing the user on high-severity items that come later. This is a genuine tradeoff with no universally correct answer. The ordering strategy partially mitigates it (high-severity first, when cognitive resources are fresh), but the fundamental tension remains. Projects with many findings will have worse Q&A quality on later items than projects with few findings. — [First principles: thoroughness and decision quality are in tension when the review set is large. This is the same tension that medical triage, code review, and audit processes all face. The standard mitigation is tiered review: full discussion for high-priority items, batch confirmation for low-priority items. The current spec rejects this approach ("no severity-based skipping"), which is a valid design choice but accepts the fatigue risk.]

- **User-initiated items (EU-02) risk scope creep if the change set format treats them identically to report-driven items.** The spec correctly gives USER_INITIATED entries the same structure as report-driven entries. However, report-driven items have upstream traceability (finding ID, affected capabilities from the scan). User-initiated items lack this provenance -- they emerge from the conversation. If change-application treats them with equal confidence, it may execute changes that were conversational asides rather than deliberate decisions. The mitigation is in the change set format: USER_INITIATED entries should require explicit confirmation ("You want this applied as a change?") before being written, which the current spec's AskUserQuestion pattern supports but doesn't explicitly mandate. — [First principles: items with upstream traceability (scan finding -> root cause -> recommendation -> user accept) have higher epistemic confidence than items without it (user mentions something in conversation). The format should encode this distinction even though the downstream execution treats them the same.]
