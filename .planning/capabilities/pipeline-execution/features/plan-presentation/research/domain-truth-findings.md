## Domain Truth Findings

**Dimension:** Domain Truth
**Date:** 2026-03-04
**Confidence:** HIGH (research literature + first-principles reasoning grounded in GSD domain research)

### First Principles

- **FP-1: A plan without justification is indistinguishable from a guess.** When a planner presents tasks in a flat table, the user has no way to evaluate whether the ordering, wave structure, or scope choices were made for principled reasons or arbitrary ones. From the user's perspective, "Wave 1: these tasks, Wave 2: those tasks" is identical whether it reflects genuine dependency analysis or random grouping. The justification narrative is not decoration — it is the evidence that the plan is reasoned, not hallucinated. [First principles: a decision without visible reasoning provides no signal about whether the reasoner understood the problem. The user cannot distinguish correct reasoning from confident-sounding guessing unless the reasoning chain is exposed.]

- **FP-2: Surfaces of a plan that the planner found ambiguous are precisely where user input has highest value.** The planner's self-critique identifies points where the planner made an assumption, chose between interpretable options, or encountered tension between requirements. These are not embarrassing failures — they are the plan's decision nodes. Hiding the self-critique result (e.g., "3 issues fixed") conceals the information the user most needs to evaluate whether the plan reflects their intent. [First principles: in any collaborative decision-making system, the highest-value human input occurs at points of genuine uncertainty. Certainty requires no review; uncertainty requires it. Surfacing only the final output without the uncertainty map deprives the reviewer of leverage.]

- **FP-3: A skippable Q&A is functionally equivalent to no Q&A.** When an interactive review step is conditioned on "unresolved checker findings," a plan that satisfies the checker with zero issues will skip the deep-dive entirely. But the user's most important questions about a plan are often not findable by automated checkers: "Why did you choose this ordering?", "Did you consider X approach?", "What's the risk if requirement Y turns out to mean something different?" These questions require human curiosity, not automated rules. Conditioning the Q&A on checker state means the better the plan, the less opportunity for human scrutiny. [First principles: gatekeeping human review behind automated pass/fail conflates "checker satisfaction" with "user satisfaction." These are orthogonal signals. A plan can pass every automated check and still represent choices the user would reject if given the opportunity to ask about them.]

- **FP-4: Plausible-looking plans create over-trust in proportion to their coherence.** Empirical research on LLM planners (N=248) found that "plausible plans generated by LLMs can mislead users into trusting the LLM agents when they are in fact wrong." A flat table of tasks with wave numbers, requirement IDs, and file paths reads as highly credible — the structure creates an appearance of rigor regardless of whether the underlying choices are sound. The justification layer is the countermeasure: it makes the reasoning visible, and visible reasoning can be interrogated, while opaque structure can only be rubber-stamped. — [Plan-Then-Execute empirical study, CHI 2025: https://arxiv.org/html/2502.01390]

- **FP-5: The "considered but rejected" dimension of a plan contains as much information as the "chosen" dimension.** Architecture Decision Record (ADR) methodology codifies this: the considered options with their pros and cons are crucial to understand the reasons for choosing a particular design. A plan that shows only what it chose cannot be evaluated against what it did not choose. If the planner considered two approaches and rejected one, that rejection is a constraint on the plan's assumptions — if the user's context invalidates the rejection reason, the whole approach may need revision. — [ADR Templates — adr.github.io: https://adr.github.io/adr-templates/; MADR project emphasis on tradeoff surfacing]

### Universal Constraints

- **UC-1: LLM-generated explanations improve plan evaluation accuracy but do not reliably calibrate trust.** Experimental research found that explanation-equipped LLM planners helped users evaluate individual plans more accurately, but failed to correct over-trust in plausible-but-wrong plans overall. The implication: a justification narrative improves the user's ability to scrutinize the plan, but does NOT replace the need for an interactive Q&A where the user can probe specific decisions. Justification + dialogue is required; justification alone is insufficient. — [Evaluating Human Trust in LLM-Based Planners, arxiv 2502.20284: https://arxiv.org/abs/2502.20284]

- **UC-2: Under high cognitive load, multi-attribute decisions degenerate into sequential single-attribute evaluation, destroying the ability to make appropriate trade-offs.** When users face a flat table of N tasks with M requirements and P dependencies, the cognitive complexity of simultaneously evaluating ordering, coverage, and approach logic exceeds working memory. Decisions become sequential ("this task looks fine... this one looks fine...") rather than holistic, with later items receiving less scrutiny due to fatigue. A structured 3-layer presentation (narrative → surfaced decisions → interactive deep-dive) progressively stages the cognitive demand. — [Cognitive Load and Decision-Making Efficiency: https://gc-bs.org/articles/the-impact-of-cognitive-load-on-decision-making-efficiency/; Phase 04 domain research DR-4 on serial presentation and decision fatigue]

- **UC-3: Automation bias is mitigated by verifiability — the ability to inspect the reasoning behind a recommendation.** Research on AI decision support systems establishes that automation bias (accepting AI output without scrutiny) is reduced when the system's reasoning is made inspectable and when users are put in an accountability posture ("justify your decision"). The justification narrative and interactive Q&A serve this function: they force the reviewing agent (the user) to engage with the reasoning, not just approve the output. — [Automation Bias — Wikipedia synthesis; https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3240751/; Addressing Automation Bias through Verifiability, CEUR-WS Vol-3442]

- **UC-4: The "why not" dimension of a plan cannot be surfaced post-hoc.** The planner has the full research context at plan creation time. By the time the user sees the plan, the planner's working memory is gone (subagent context is isolated and not preserved). If the planner does not record "I considered approach X and rejected it because Y" during plan creation, that rationale is permanently lost. Surfacing considered-but-rejected decisions requires a deliberate mechanism built into the planner's self-critique output format — it cannot be reconstructed later. [First principles: LLM subagents have ephemeral context (established in Phase 02 Domain Truth, Truth 8). Information not written to disk during the agent's execution window is permanently lost. Planner rationale must be captured during the planning step, not reconstructed during the presentation step.]

- **UC-5: Human involvement in the planning stage improves task performance without necessarily changing trust calibration.** The CHI 2025 plan-then-execute study found that user involvement in planning "can be beneficial in addressing imperfect plans and fixing execution errors, resulting in better task performance" — but this benefit depends on the interaction being meaningful, not perfunctory. An "approve?" prompt with no mechanism for deep engagement gives the appearance of user involvement without delivering its benefits. The interactive Q&A is not about trust management — it is about surfacing errors in the plan before execution. — [Plan-Then-Execute, CHI 2025: https://arxiv.org/html/2502.01390]

### Validated Assumptions

- **VA-1: Visual structure (ASCII flow diagrams) reduces parse time for plan architecture but does not replace narrative.** Multimedia and presentation research consistently shows that visual representations reduce cognitive load for structural/sequential information — understanding how Wave 1 feeds Wave 2 is faster from a dependency diagram than from prose. However, visual structure conveys what, not why. The plan narrative (prose explaining ordering logic, approach rationale, simplicity choices) carries information that cannot be represented in a flow chart. Both are needed: the diagram reduces parse cost, the narrative carries reasoning. [First principles: visual representation encodes topology (connections, order, hierarchy); prose encodes causality and justification. These are not substitutable.]

- **VA-2: The ADR pattern (context → decision → alternatives considered → consequences) is the established domain solution for surfacing decision rationale in software planning.** Architecture Decision Records are the canonical method for recording why a software decision was made rather than alternatives. The MADR template explicitly includes "considered options" with pros/cons as required fields, precisely because "understanding why a design was chosen requires examining what was not chosen." The GSD plan justification layer maps directly onto ADR's decision + alternatives structure, applied at plan/approach level rather than at architecture level. — [ADR best practices — AWS: https://docs.aws.amazon.com/prescriptive-guidance/latest/architectural-decision-records/adr-process.html; MADR: https://adr.github.io/adr-templates/]

- **VA-3: Interactive refinement produces higher user confidence than passive explanation alone, independent of evaluation accuracy.** The preliminary trust study found that refinement capability "showed promise as a trust-building mechanism" where "users value the ability to improve solutions even if their assessment accuracy remains unchanged." Applied to GSD: the interactive Q&A deep-dive is not just a scrutiny mechanism — it creates the psychological condition of collaborative ownership, where users feel the plan reflects their input rather than having been handed to them. This ownership effect matters for commitment to execution. — [Evaluating Human Trust in LLM-Based Planners, arxiv 2502.20284: https://arxiv.org/abs/2502.20284]

### Domain Risks

- **DR-1: The justification narrative may itself exhibit the plausibility trap — a well-written rationale for a bad plan is more dangerous than no rationale at all.** Research shows plausible-seeming plans are harder for users to reject than obviously incomplete ones. A fluent justification narrative increases plan plausibility, which could increase approval of flawed plans rather than decrease it. Risk: the narrative layer adds polish that amplifies rubber-stamping behavior. Mitigation: the narrative must be grounded in verifiable claims (specific requirement IDs, actual dependency edges, file references) not generic justification-sounding prose. Grounded claims are interrogatable; fluent prose is not. — [Plan-Then-Execute plausibility trap finding, CHI 2025: https://arxiv.org/html/2502.01390; First principles: verifiability as the core mitigant for automation bias, not explanation quality alone.]

- **DR-2: If the interactive Q&A deep-dive is offered but the user declines, the system has still improved on the current state.** The feature goal requires the Q&A is "always offered (never skipped)." The domain risk is treating "always offered" as equivalent to "always used." The system must offer the deep-dive unconditionally, but the user can decline. The offer itself matters: it signals that the plan has decision nodes worth examining and that the user has the ability to influence them. Not offering it at all (current behavior when checker finds no issues) is the failure mode, not the user declining it when offered. [First principles: the existence of an opt-out does not negate the value of the default opt-in. The current behavior is: no Q&A when checker passes → user cannot distinguish "no issues" from "checker didn't look for this". New behavior: Q&A always offered → user can always probe, even when checker passes.]

- **DR-3: The self-critique surfacing must distinguish between "decisions the planner made confidently" and "decisions the planner was uncertain about."** Round 1 of self-critique fixes issues silently. Round 2 surfaces remaining issues. But neither round explicitly surfaces confident decisions that the user might nonetheless want to interrogate — e.g., "I chose wave-parallel execution over sequential because dependency analysis showed no coupling" is a confident decision, but one the user might want to question. The risk is that surfaced decisions = only uncertain decisions, while the most consequential confident decisions remain hidden. Mitigation: the planner's justification layer should include at least the key confident approach decisions, not just the uncertain ones. [First principles: confident decisions and uncertain decisions both represent planner-authority over choices. The user should have visibility into both categories, with the ability to probe either. Surfacing only uncertainty creates an incomplete picture of where planner judgment was exercised.]

- **DR-4: Adding justification content increases the token cost of the plan checkpoint presentation, creating pressure against thoroughness.** The plan checkpoint currently presents flat tables — low token count, fast to render. A 3-layer presentation (narrative + surfaced decisions + Q&A offer) requires substantially more content. In a context-window-constrained environment, the temptation is to abbreviate the narrative or reduce the number of surfaced decisions to keep presentation cost down. Risk: the justification layer degrades to a few boilerplate sentences that add words without adding information. Mitigation: the narrative should be bounded (paragraph, not essay) and the surfaced decisions should be enumerated (3-5 key decisions, not a comprehensive log). [First principles: context windows are finite and competitive (Phase 02 Domain Truth, Truth 2). The presentation layer must earn its token cost through information density, not through coverage exhaustiveness.]
